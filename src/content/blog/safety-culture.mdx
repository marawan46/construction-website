Three years ago, our safety record was average. Not bad enough to cause alarm, not good enough to be proud of. We had the toolbox talks, the PPE policies, the incident report forms. What we didn't have was a crew that believed safety was theirs to own — not management's to enforce.

That changed after a near-miss on a steel erection job in early 2022. Nobody was hurt. But it shook us enough to ask a harder question: why, despite all our procedures, did this nearly happen?

The answer wasn't a missing policy. It was a missing culture.

## What "Safety Culture" Actually Means

Safety culture is not a poster on the site office wall. It's what your crew does when nobody is watching. It's whether a labourer speaks up when they see an unsafe lift, or stays quiet because they don't want to slow the job down.

<Callout type="insight">
  The single biggest predictor of a site's safety record isn't its procedures — it's whether frontline workers feel psychologically safe enough to raise concerns without fear of embarrassment or retaliation.
</Callout>

Culture is built through repeated behaviours over time. Which means it can be deliberately shaped — but it takes longer than a training session.

## Where We Started



The increase in near-miss reports is the number we're proudest of. More reports doesn't mean more incidents — it means people stopped hiding close calls and started treating them as learning opportunities.

## The Peer Observer Model

The biggest structural change was replacing top-down safety audits with a peer observer program. Instead of a site manager ticking boxes on a clipboard, we trained six crew members per site to conduct informal safety conversations with their colleagues during the shift.

These aren't inspections. They're conversations. "I noticed you were working close to the edge there — walk me through your thinking." That's a very different dynamic than a manager pointing out a violation.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Peer observers work because trust travels horizontally.** A labourer is more likely to have an honest conversation with a coworker than with the person who signs their timesheet.
</Callout>

## What Still Needs Work

We haven't solved everything. Subcontractor integration remains a challenge — it's harder to embed culture in crews who aren't with you every day. We're currently piloting a joint onboarding session that brings our crews and subcontractor crews through the same safety induction together, rather than separately.

The data is improving. But culture isn't a destination. It's a practice.
