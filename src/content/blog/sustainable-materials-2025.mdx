The conversation around sustainable construction has matured. Five years ago, it was mostly greenwashing — marketing language attached to projects that were conventional in every meaningful way. Today, a handful of materials are genuinely changing what we build and how.

We've spent the last two years trialling alternatives on live projects. Here's our unfiltered take.

## Mass Timber: The Real Deal

Cross-laminated timber (CLT) and glulam have moved well past novelty. We used CLT on our Southbank office fit-out last year and came away impressed — not just for its environmental credentials, but for practical reasons that matter on site.

The speed advantage is real. Prefabricated CLT panels arrive on site ready to install — no formwork, no curing time. On a tight urban site with restricted working hours, that matters enormously.

## Recycled Aggregate: Good Enough, Not Great

We've been using recycled crushed concrete aggregate (RCA) in non-structural applications for three years. It works well in road base, drainage layers, and landscape fill. For structural concrete? The evidence is still patchy.

The variability in RCA quality is the core problem. Without tight supply chain controls, you can't guarantee consistent particle size distribution, which makes mix design unpredictable. We've had good results when sourcing from certified recyclers, but the market isn't mature enough yet for blanket confidence.

## Low-Carbon Concrete: Where the Volume Is

For sheer impact, low-carbon concrete mixes — using supplementary cementitious materials like fly ash or ground granulated blast-furnace slag (GGBS) to partially replace Portland cement — offer the best return on effort.

Cement production accounts for roughly 8% of global CO₂ emissions. Replacing 40–70% of cement with GGBS in a mix can cut the concrete's embodied carbon by a similar proportion, with minimal performance trade-offs if designed correctly.

We've standardised on 50% GGBS replacement across all non-time-critical pours. The only adjustment is extending curing time slightly, since GGBS mixes gain strength more slowly than pure OPC.

## What's Still Hype

**Hempcrete** gets a lot of press. It has genuine applications in thermal mass wall construction, particularly in low-rise residential. But its compressive strength is too low for most commercial work, and the supply chain in most markets is not yet reliable at volume.

**Mycelium composites** are fascinating in a laboratory context. On a live construction project in 2025, they are not a specification decision.

<Callout type="warning">
    Don't let a sustainability brief push you into using RCA in structural pours without rigorous mix testing. The embodied carbon savings are real, but not worth a compromised specification.
</Callout>

## Our Current Standard Spec

For a typical commercial build today, our default position is: CLT or glulam wherever the structure permits it, 50% GGBS concrete for all in-situ pours, and RCA for all sub-base and non-structural fill. That combination, across a mid-size project, typically cuts embodied carbon by 35–45% compared to a conventional specification — without adding cost or programme time.

The technology is there. The barrier now is familiarity, not feasibility.
