Construction contracts are long documents written by lawyers for lawyers. Nobody expects a site manager or a project director to enjoy reading them. But the clauses that hurt you most are rarely the dramatic ones — they're the quiet, administrative ones buried in the middle that determine who bears the cost when things go sideways.

Here's what we look for before we sign anything.

## The Payment Terms

This is where most subcontractors get into trouble. Look for three things: the payment schedule (milestone-based or progress-based?), the timeframe for payment after claim submission, and whether there's a "pay when paid" clause.

<Callout type="warning">
  **"Pay when paid" clauses** make your payment contingent on the head contractor receiving payment from the client. In some jurisdictions these are unenforceable, but they still appear in contracts. Know the law in your state before accepting one.
</Callout>

The payment timeframe matters more than people realise. A 30-day vs. 45-day payment window on a $2M subcontract represents a material difference in cash flow — especially if claims are disputed and the clock resets.

## Variations Procedure

How contract variations are handled is the single biggest source of construction disputes. The contract will specify a procedure: written instruction required, timeframe for submitting a variation claim, timeframe for the principal to respond.

The problem isn't usually the procedure — it's that crews do variation work before the instruction is formalised because the job needs to keep moving. Then the claim comes in and the principal disputes it because the procedure wasn't followed.

<Callout type="tip">
  Establish a site-level habit from day one: **no variation work starts until a written direction is issued**, even if it's just an email from the superintendent. A 20-minute delay to get something in writing is worth it every time.
</Callout>

## Risk and Extension of Time Provisions


Extension of time (EOT) clauses govern what happens when the project is delayed by events outside your control — weather, sovereign risk, variations instructed by the client. Most contracts require you to give written notice of a delay event within a defined window (commonly 14–28 days of becoming aware of it). Miss that window and you may lose your right to claim the extension, even if the delay was entirely the client's fault.

Read this clause carefully. Know the trigger date and the notice period. Then build a site diary habit that documents delay events as they happen.

## Liquidated Damages

Liquidated damages (LDs) are the daily penalty rate you pay if you overrun the completion date. They're common and generally enforceable. Two things to check:

First, is the rate reasonable? Courts in most jurisdictions will only enforce LDs that represent a genuine pre-estimate of the principal's loss. An eye-wateringly high LD rate may indicate the contract is designed to be punitive rather than compensatory — which is a red flag about the client's intent.

Second, does the contract cap total LDs? An uncapped LD clause on a tight programme is a significant financial exposure. Push to cap them at 10–15% of contract value if they aren't already.

## Termination for Convenience

Check whether the principal has a unilateral right to terminate the contract for convenience — meaning without needing to establish that you've done anything wrong. Most commercial contracts include this. The question is: what are you entitled to if they exercise it?

<Callout type="important">
  You should be entitled to payment for all work completed to the termination date, plus reasonable demobilisation costs, plus loss of profit on the remaining works. If the termination clause excludes loss of profit on remaining works, that's a negotiation point before you sign.
</Callout>

## One Final Rule

No matter how well you know the client, do not rely on a relationship to manage contract risk. The people you're dealing with today may not be there when a dispute arises two years from now. The contract is what remains. Make sure it says what you intend it to say — before the first shovel hits the ground.
